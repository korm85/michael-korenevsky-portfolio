# PM Portfolio Review
**Reviewer perspective:** Head of PM evaluating Michael Korenevsky's portfolio  
**Goal:** Identify what reads as genuine PM achievement vs. product marketing copy

---

## Summary

The portfolio has strong bones. The "Key Decisions" sections and first-person ownership narratives are already PM-voiced. The main pattern to fix: anywhere the copy describes what the *product* does, it should instead describe what *you decided* and what happened as a result.

---

## Feedback Items

### [FB-01] Hero tagline is generic martech copy

**Location:** Hero section, subtitle  
**Current text:**  
> "Building complex products from scratch, owning the end-to-end process to transform complex data into actionable insights."

**Issue:** "Transform complex data into actionable insights" could describe 500 PM portfolios. A Head of PM skimming this has no idea what makes Michael different in the first 3 seconds.

**Opportunity:** The QA→PM identity buried at the bottom of the career timeline is the real differentiator. That angle — a decade of edge-case thinking applied to AI product decisions — should be the hook here.

**Priority:** High — first impression

---

### [FB-02] Project card headlines are product taglines, not PM positioning

**Location:** SelectedWork — AMVero card headline and Simulation card headline  
**Current text:**  
- AMVero: "Real-time AI quality control for industrial manufacturing"  
- Simulation: "Predictive simulation that lets clients ship right the first time"

**Issue:** These describe what the *product* does, not what Michael *contributed* to it. A Head of PM reads these and immediately asks "So what was your role?" The headline should answer that before they have to ask.

**Opportunity:** Reframe as PM ownership statements — lead with what Michael drove, not what the product delivers.

**Priority:** High — reframes every card from "product" to "person"

---

### [FB-03] Outcome metrics lack PM attribution

**Location:** Metric boxes on both project cards  
**Current text (examples):**  
- "98% | Reduction in active monitoring time"  
- "18% | Scrap cost reduction"  
- "80% | Fewer manufacturing errors"

**Issue:** These read as product outcomes, not PM-caused outcomes. Without a line from Michael's decision to the number, a Head of PM can't tell if he drove these results or just measured them.

**Opportunity:** Add a short attribution clause that connects each metric to a specific decision Michael made. Not a long explanation — one phrase is enough.

**Example reframe:**  
- Before: "98% | Reduction in active monitoring time"  
- After: "98% | Reduction in review time — driven by the condition-based filtering architecture I specified"

**Priority:** High — attribution is what separates PM achievement from product measurement

---

### [FB-04] AMVero modal "PM Contribution" conflates process with outcome

**Location:** AMVero interactive prototype modal, "PM Contribution" section  
**Current text:**  
> "Owned the product backlog end-to-end: managed Scrum sprints to sign 5 enterprise customers (e.g. Baker Hughes, Thales) in 5 months. Defined warning thresholds and alerting logic to mitigate operator alarm fatigue, curating training data to improve model accuracy to 90%."

**Issue:** "Managed Scrum sprints to sign 5 enterprise customers" conflates sprint methodology with a sales outcome — a Head of PM will notice these aren't causally linked. The sprint management didn't sign customers; the alerting design and deployment playbook did.

**Opportunity:** Rewrite around decisions and trade-offs Michael made, not activities he performed. What was the alerting threshold decision? What data did the training curation change? These are the PM story.

**Priority:** Medium — modal is secondary real estate but visible to anyone who engages

---

### [FB-05] AMVero interactive prototype demo showcases the product, not the PM

**Location:** AMVero modal, right-hand interactive console  
**Current content:** Live console with machine cards, camera feeds, layer progress bars, alert status, rule creation form

**Issue:** This is an impressive product demo — but it demonstrates the product, not Michael. A Head of PM browsing it thinks "cool product" and immediately asks "but what did the PM decide here and why?" There is no answer visible in the prototype experience.

**Opportunity:** Either (a) add a PM annotation layer — callouts that explain *why* each design decision was made, or (b) reframe the prototype intro copy to lead with the decision story before the demo. The interactive element is a strength; it just needs PM context wrapped around it.

**Priority:** Medium — the demo is a differentiator, worth preserving with better framing

---

### [FB-06] "How I Work" section lists tools instead of proving capability

**Location:** HowIWork section, all four cards  
**Current headlines and copy:**  
- "Discovery & specs" — "I catch the conflicts and gaps early, so they don't turn into sprint rework."  
- "Prototyping" — "When I do, users get higher-value features in the first release."  
- "Hermes PM agent" — "I walk into strategy discussions with fresh context instead of a deck I put together last week."  
- "Support intelligence" — "My roadmap stays grounded in what customers actually get stuck on."

**Issue:** The cards describe what the tools do, not what Michael shipped faster or better because of them. "Hermes PM agent" appears with no explanation — a Head of PM won't know what this is and won't ask. The section reads as tech bragging, not PM capability.

**Opportunity:** Replace tool descriptions with impact claims. If the multi-agent setup helped ship the alerting PRD faster, say that. If competitive research changed a pricing decision, say that. Each card should answer: "So what did you do differently because of this, and what was the result?"

**Priority:** Medium — section has a clear purpose, needs outcome language instead of process language

---

### [FB-07] Career timeline entries are job descriptions, not achievements

**Location:** CareerTimeline section, each role entry  
**Current text (examples):**  
- "Leading the AI vision product for industrial manufacturing."  
- "Owned strategy, roadmap, and pricing for predictive simulation software."  
- "Built and led the QA team for industrial CAD/CAM software."

**Issue:** These are job descriptions. They describe scope of responsibility, not what was shipped or decided. A Head of PM skimming the timeline learns Michael held these roles but not what he accomplished in them.

**Opportunity:** Add one achievement hook per role — a single shipped thing, a named decision, or a number. The QA roles especially need this: the QA background is a differentiator, but "Designed validation frameworks" doesn't tell the story.

**Priority:** Medium — timeline is supporting context, but sparse entries waste the space

---

### [FB-08] QA→PM identity is buried at the bottom of the timeline

**Location:** CareerTimeline, footer note  
**Current text:**  
> "A decade of QA engineering before PM. Edge-case thinking is the foundation of how I make AI product decisions."

**Issue:** This is the most differentiated line on the entire site and it's a footnote in gray italic text at the bottom of the career section. Most visitors will never read it.

**Opportunity:** Surface this much earlier — ideally in the hero or as a framing element on the work cards. The combination of engineering rigor + PM strategy + AI products is genuinely rare. It should be the first thing a Head of PM learns about Michael, not the last.

**Priority:** High — this is Michael's clearest differentiator; it should lead, not trail

---

## What to Keep Without Changes

- **Key Decisions bullets** on both project cards — best PM-specific content on the site; show trade-off reasoning, not feature lists
- **"I took AMVero from first enterprise pilot to five paying clients in five months"** — personal ownership, time-bounded, outcome-attached
- **ROI pricing simulator** — demonstrates pricing strategy thinking interactively; very few PM portfolios show this
- **PRD pull quote + linked artifact** — anchors claims with real documents
- **Customer testimonials with named clients and specific metrics** — strong social proof with attribution
- **Card body descriptions** (the "I owned…" paragraphs) — already PM-voiced, keep the framing
